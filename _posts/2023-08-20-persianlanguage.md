The Persian language holds a special place in my heart; it's a passion that I deeply cherish. Growing up in a household adorned with numerous old books, especially poetry collections, I was captivated by the literary world. As a child, I would find solace in my own company, sitting in a quiet room, immersing myself in the verses of Rumi, Khayyam, Rudaki, and Khaqani. It was an enchanting experience that I often yearn to relive. Those were the days when I longed to venture into learning Sanskrit, driven by my fascination with Kalīla wa-Dimna which was originally written in Sanskrit. I aspired to grasp whether a writer proficient in Sanskrit perceives and conveys the world in a manner that resonates with my own experiences. 

I consider myself a true patriot, deeply devoted to my Iranian heritage. My love for the Persian language is profound. However, it's disheartening to realize that there seems to be no place left for me in Iran, and the essence of being Iranian has faded away. It's as if that beloved country, which my heart beats for, no longer truly exists. Nothing remains of that homeland; the culture has undergone a reset. People are solely focused on preserving themselves, with little regard for the environment or cultural values. The Persian language is gradually fading, leaving fewer traces for me to proudly connect to my ancestral land. Listening to songs from four decades ago fills me with excitement, but that sense of joy is largely absent today. My research sometimes grants me the opportunity to delve into the history of languages. Each time I embark on this journey, the impact of the Persian language leaves me teary-eyed. The influence this language had on art and the precious knowledge it carried during its heyday is truly remarkable.

Recently, while studying the history of the Mughal Empire in India, I was struck by the realization of how someone (Babur) born in Uzbekistan, resting in Afghanistan's mausoleum, and ruling over India with a native Turkic language, had become deeply intertwined with Iranian culture and the Persian language. If the British hadn't come into India, how different the world might have been. You never know. The intricate connections between languages, cultures, and histories never cease to amaze me. 
Again, I am reminded of the profound impact language and history have on shaping our identity and understanding of the world. As I continue this journey, I am determined to keep the flame of the Persian language alive within me, at least in my research, with the hope that I can contribute in some way.

Here, I'd like to outline my effort here:

1.  A small project of mine that suddenly becomes bigger is the creation of a highly scalable library (known as [Parstdex](https://github.com/kargaranamir/parstdex)) for extracting Persian time and date markers. An equivalent and better version of this work in English was done in 2012 (ten years ago).
We subsequently expanded upon this effort by incorporating transformers leading to its publication ([Hengam models](https://github.com/kargaranamir/Hengam)) in the Asia chapter of the ACL.

2. [Glot500](https://github.com/cisnlp/Glot500) stands as another project, with our objective centered around developing a multilingual masked language model designed to assist numerous low-resource languages. The dataset for this model encompasses a variety of languages, such as Iranian Persian, South Azerbaijani, Kurdish, Tajik, and Dari.
